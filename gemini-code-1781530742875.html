REFLECTIVE ESSAY

Introduction

When I first joined this Minor, I wasn’t entirely sure what kind of educator I wanted to become. By the end of it, I have a much clearer picture — and a much more honest one. This essay reflects on what I have actually learned, where I think my strengths lie, and what I still need to work on. My plan is to teach English, most likely at a language school or secondary level, and eventually to work in a context where I can design my own courses rather than just follow a pre-set syllabus. What appeals to me about teaching is the combination of intellectual challenge and direct human interaction — no two lessons, and no two students, are ever quite the same.

Educational Practices Learned

Three things from the Minor genuinely changed how I think about teaching. The first is language analysis. Before this course, I would have looked at a grammar point and thought “I know this — I can explain it.” Now I know that explaining is actually the last step, not the first. Learning to break down meaning, form and pronunciation separately — and to anticipate where students will struggle — made me realise how much preparation good teaching actually requires. The second is lesson staging. The Didactics course showed me that a lesson isn’t just a sequence of activities; it has a logic to it, and when that logic is missing, students feel lost even if the activities themselves are good. Third, the learner analysis task genuinely surprised me. Writing a detailed profile of one student made me see how many assumptions teachers make without realising it — and how much those assumptions can get in the way.

Strengths as a Future Educator

I find it easier to talk about weaknesses than strengths, but I’ll try to be honest here rather than just modest. I think I communicate clearly — I notice when people look confused and I naturally try different ways of explaining something rather than just repeating myself louder. I am also genuinely interested in language, which helps: I get curious about why things work the way they do in English, and I think that curiosity is something students can pick up on. Finally, I think I am reasonably good at reading a room — at noticing when the energy drops, or when someone is lost but not saying so. Whether that translates into good classroom management is something I still need to find out, but it feels like a useful starting point.

Professional Development Plan

AREAS TO WORK ON WHAT I AM GOING TO DO WHY THIS WILL HELP
Giving clear instructions I will script my instructions in advance, design clear demonstrations, and prepare instruction-checking questions (ICQs) before each lesson. Scripted instructions will help me identify ambiguities before the lesson and ensure students understand exactly what to do, reducing off-task time.
Classroom management I will observe experienced colleagues teaching, take notes on how they manage transitions, noise levels, and student attention, and experiment with these strategies in my own teaching practice. Observing skilled practitioners and reflecting on what works will help me develop a toolkit of management strategies tailored to different learner profiles and class sizes.
Assessment and feedback I will enroll in a short course on formative assessment, study feedback models (e.g., written corrective feedback in EFL), and begin using error logs with students to track recurring mistakes. Understanding how to give meaningful, timely feedback will help students improve more rapidly and feel supported in their learning journey.

Conclusion

Looking back, this Minor was more demanding than I expected — and more useful. I came in thinking about teaching as something intuitive, something you either have a feel for or you don’t. I leave it understanding that it’s also a craft: something that can be studied, broken down, practised and improved. I’m not a confident teacher yet, but I feel like I’m starting from somewhere real rather than somewhere vague, and that makes a difference.

LANGUAGE ANALYSES

Grammar Language Analysis: Present Perfect with "never"

TARGET LANGUAGE Present Perfect with never e.g. I have never been to Britain.
MEANING ANALYSIS Used to talk about life experiences — things that have or have not happened at any point up to now. It expresses that an experience has not occurred in one's lifetime.
ELICITATION Context: Tell students about an adventurous friend, then mention one thing she has never done.
Questions: Has my friend ever jumped with a parachute? (No) | When didn't she jump? (At no time in her life)
CONCEPT CHECKING CCQs: Did she jump with a parachute last year? (No) | Did she jump at any time in her life? (No) | Is it possible she might jump in the future? (Yes)
FORM
Subject + have/has + never + past participle
I/You/We/They + have never + past participle
He/She/It + has never + past participle
PRONUNCIATION have /həv/ and has /həz/ (weakly stressed). never receives strong stress: /ˈnevər/. Falling intonation at end: I have NEVER been to BRItain.
WRITTEN RECORD Elicit Meaning, Form, Pronunciation onto the board for notebooks. Color coding: have/has, never, past participle.
PROBLEMS & SOLUTIONS P: Confusion with simple past negative (*didn't). S: Use CCQs to anchor to lifetime scope.
P: Omission of auxiliary or wrong verb form (*I never went / *I have never go). S: Substitution drills and strict timeline display.

Vocabulary Language Analysis: Mood and Food

LEXICAL SET relaxed (calm, not worried) | stressed (under pressure) | focused (paying full attention) | depressed (very sad) | aggressive (angry/violent)
FORM & SYNTAX Adjectives. Predicative (I feel stressed) or Attributive (a focused student).
PRONUNCIATION relaxed /rɪˈlækst/ [2nd] | stressed /strest/ [1 syl] | focused /ˈfəʊkəst/ [1st] | depressed /dɪˈprest/ [2nd] | aggressive /əˈɡresɪv/ [2nd]
DIFFERENTIATION Problem: Confusing 'stressed' and 'depressed'. Solution: Use a semantic scale/cline on board (mildly worried → stressed → depressed).

LEARNER ANALYSIS

Learner Profile: Olga, 18 yrs | Context: Bilingual (RU/DE), Academic English Need

Olga is bilingual in Russian and German. At home she mostly uses Russian, and at school she often uses German. She encounters English mostly in songs, short videos, game interfaces and memes, and frequently uses Russian subtitles. She needs English mainly for university, to travel, and to talk to foreign friends.

ANALYSIS SPECIFIC LEARNING NEEDS
Receptive & Productive Skills Olga gets the gist of listening tasks, but fast speech and unfamiliar accents are challenging. In speaking, she is comfortable in pairs but struggles with longer independent monologues. In writing, she produces functional short texts but makes many grammar errors due to literal translation.
  • Develop reading fluency without constant mental translation.
  • Practice extended discourse and structured independent monologues.
  • Expand active vocabulary to reduce reliance on L1/L2 interference.
Grammar, Vocabulary & Pronunciation Olga applies Russian or German word order instead of English syntax patterns. Her passive vocabulary is noticeably larger than her active production. Pronunciation is intelligible but heavily influenced by German/Russian vowel quality and word stress patterns.
  • Isolate and practice strict English word order templates.
  • Activate passive vocabulary via targeted low-stakes collocations.
  • Practice word and sentence stress layout.

Duration: 60 min | Level: B1 | Class Size: 12 | Materials: Handouts, Board, Cards
