Overview

Executive Summary

This portfolio has been compiled to document my professional growth, teaching skills, and developing educational philosophy as a future language educator. It serves as a comprehensive record of the competencies and practical experiences I have gained throughout the Minor in Language Culture and Intercultural Communication. The portfolio reflects my commitment to becoming a reflective and effective educator, and demonstrates my readiness to apply evidence-based language teaching practices.

The portfolio includes a range of materials that together provide a holistic view of my development as an educator. These include a reflective essay, grammar and vocabulary language analyses, a self-designed learning project and extracurricular event, a learner analysis assignment, controlled and freer language practice tasks, activities for an authentic text, and a lesson plan. Each component has been carefully prepared to illustrate my understanding of language teaching methodology, learner needs, and instructional design. Taken as a whole, these documents demonstrate my skills, creativity, and dedication to creating a rich, meaningful, and engaging learning environment for all students.

Reflection

Reflective Essay

Introduction

When I first joined this Minor, I wasn’t entirely sure what kind of educator I wanted to become. By the end of it, I have a much clearer picture — and a much more honest one. This essay reflects on what I have actually learned, where I think my strengths lie, and what I still need to work on. My plan is to teach English, most likely at a language school or secondary level, and eventually to work in a context where I can design my own courses rather than just follow a pre-set syllabus. What appeals to me about teaching is the combination of intellectual challenge and direct human interaction — no two lessons, and no two students, are ever quite the same.

Educational Practices Learned

Three things from the Minor genuinely changed how I think about teaching. The first is language analysis. Before this course, I would have looked at a grammar point and thought “I know this — I can explain it.” Now I know that explaining is actually the last step, not the first. Learning to break down meaning, form and pronunciation separately — and to anticipate where students will struggle — made me realise how much preparation good teaching actually requires. The second is lesson staging. The Didactics course showed me that a lesson isn’t just a sequence of activities; it has a logic to it, and when that logic is missing, students feel lost even if the activities themselves are good. Third, the learner analysis task genuinely surprised me. Writing a detailed profile of one student made me see how many assumptions teachers make without realising it — and how much those assumptions can get in the way.

Strengths as a Future Educator

I find it easier to talk about weaknesses than strengths, but I’ll try to be honest here rather than just modest. I think I communicate clearly — I notice when people look confused and I naturally try different ways of explaining something rather than just repeating myself louder. I am also genuinely interested in language, which helps: I get curious about why things work the way they do in English, and I think that curiosity is something students can pick up on. Finally, I think I am reasonably good at reading a room — at noticing when the energy drops, or when someone is lost but not saying so. Whether that translates into good classroom management is something I still need to find out, but it feels like a useful starting point.

Professional Development Plan

Tbl. 01 — Development Plan03 / Reflective Essay
Areas to Work On What I Am Going to Do Why This Will Help
Giving clear instructions I will script my instructions in advance, design clear demonstrations, and prepare instruction-checking questions (ICQs) before each lesson. Scripted instructions will help me identify ambiguities before the lesson and ensure students understand exactly what to do, reducing off-task time.
Classroom management techniques I will observe experienced colleagues teaching, take notes on how they manage transitions, noise levels, and student attention, and experiment with these strategies in my own teaching practice. Observing skilled practitioners and reflecting on what works will help me develop a toolkit of management strategies tailored to different learner profiles and class sizes.
Assessment and feedback skills I will enroll in a short course on formative assessment, study feedback models (e.g., written corrective feedback in EFL), and begin using error logs with students to track recurring mistakes. Understanding how to give meaningful, timely feedback will help students improve more rapidly and feel supported in their learning journey.

Conclusion

Looking back, this Minor was more demanding than I expected — and more useful. I came in thinking about teaching as something intuitive, something you either have a feel for or you don’t. I leave it understanding that it’s also a craft: something that can be studied, broken down, practised and improved. I’m not a confident teacher yet, but I feel like I’m starting from somewhere real rather than somewhere vague, and that makes a difference.

Grammar Language Analysis

Tbl. 02 — Grammar AnalysisPresent Perfect with never
TARGET LANGUAGE What is the language point?Example: Present Perfect with nevere.g. I have never been to Britain.
Meaning Analysis The Present Perfect with never is used to talk about life experiences — things that have or have not happened at any point up to now. It expresses that an experience has not occurred in one’s lifetime.
Elicitation: Meaning Context: I will tell students about a friend who is very adventurous and then mention that despite this, there is one thing she has never done.Elicitation questions:– Has my friend ever jumped with a parachute? (No)– When didn’t she jump? (At no time in her life)
Concept Checking CCQs:– Did she jump with a parachute last year? (No)– Did she jump at any time in her life? (No)– Is it possible she might jump in the future? (Yes)
Form Affirmative: Subject + have/has + never + past participleI / You / We / They + have never + past participleHe / She / It + has never + past participlee.g. I have never been to Britain. / She has never read Harry Potter.
Pronunciation have /həv/ and has /həz/ are weakly stressed in the middle of a sentence.never receives strong stress: /ˈnevər/Falling intonation at end of statement: I have NEVER been to BRItain.
Written Record I will elicit Meaning, Form, and Pronunciation onto the board. Students will copy from the board into their notebooks. Key parts of the form will be highlighted in colour (have/has in blue, never in red, past participle underlined).
Anticipated Problems & Solutions: Meaning Problem 1: Ss may confuse ‘never’ with a simple past negative (e.g. didn’t).Solution 1: Use CCQs to clarify that we are talking about all of life up to now, not a single past moment.Problem 2: Ss may think ‘never’ is only used for permanent states.Solution 2: Provide varied examples showing temporary/changeable situations (travel, food, activities).
Anticipated Problems & Solutions: Form Problem 1: Ss may omit have/has or use the wrong auxiliary (e.g. *I never went).Solution 1: Highlight the full form on the board. Elicit more examples and drill.Problem 2: Ss may use the infinitive instead of the past participle (e.g. *I have never go).Solution 2: Provide a list of irregular past participles and run a substitution drill.
Anticipated Problems & Solutions: Phonology Problem 1: Ss may over-stress have/has, making it sound unnatural.Solution 1: Model the weak form and contrast: ‘I HAVE NEVER’ (overemphasised) vs ‘I’ve never’ (natural). Drill chorally.
Reference Materials Used Swan, M. (2005). Practical English Usage (3rd ed.). Oxford University Press. (pp. 230–238) Murphy, R. (2019). English Grammar in Use (5th ed.). Cambridge University Press. (Unit 17–18)

Project · The Ultimate Eco-Travel Guide

Self-Designed Learning Project

Project Title

The Ultimate Eco-Travel Guide

Teacher: Lashkov Ilya Format: Pairs Level: A2–B1 Time: 3 mins Deadline: May 31, 2026

Learner Profile & Rationale

The target group consists of 12 pre-intermediate to intermediate (A2–B1 level) ESL students aged 14–15. They are interested in travel, foreign cultures, and social media such as vlogs, TikTok, and Instagram. The small class size allows the teacher to support each pair during teamwork. The project theme — Eco-Tourism and Hidden Travel Gems — was chosen to connect with their natural interests while building descriptive and presentational language skills.

Project Task & Final Product

In pairs, students choose a less famous but beautiful travel destination and create a simple 3-day eco-friendly travel plan. The final product is a short digital presentation (Canva or PowerPoint) or a simple video vlog. Students present for 3 minutes and answer 1–2 questions from the class.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this project, students will be able to:

  • Use basic travel and nature vocabulary correctly while speaking.
  • Find and use simple information from English travel websites.
  • Work together in pairs to create an organised digital travel presentation.
  • Give a clear 3-minute spoken presentation to recommend their travel destination.

Project · My English Vocabulary Journal

Self-Designed Learning Project

Project Title

My English Vocabulary Journal: A 4-Week Self-Study Project

Aims & Rationale

This project was designed to help learners with a larger passive than active vocabulary begin using new lexis productively. The target learner (Olga, profiled in the Learner Analysis section) encounters English regularly through media but rarely produces it. This project addresses that gap by creating structured, low-stakes daily practice.

Project Description

The learner keeps a vocabulary journal over four weeks. Each week has a theme (Week 1: Emotions; Week 2: Daily Routines; Week 3: Technology; Week 4: Food and Health). Each day, the learner records 3–5 new words or collocations encountered naturally (e.g. in a song, video, or game), notes the meaning, part of speech, and an example sentence, and attempts to use the words in a short written or spoken task.

Weekly Schedule

Tbl. 08 — Weekly Schedule08 / Vocabulary Journal
WeekTheme & FocusOutput Task
1Emotions — adjectives describing mood and feelingsWrite a short diary entry using 5 new words
2Daily Routines — collocations and phrasesRecord a 1-minute voice memo describing your morning
3Technology — tech vocabulary and common expressionsWrite a social-media-style post about a tech product
4Food & Health — nouns, verbs and collocationsPresent ‘my perfect meal’ to a partner in 2 minutes

Assessment

The project is assessed on consistency (completing daily entries), quality of output tasks, and reflection (a short written self-evaluation at the end of Week 4). No formal test is included — the focus is on building autonomous learning habits.

Extracurricular Event Description

Event Title

English Movie Night: ‘Friends’ Pronunciation Club

Rationale

This event was designed to make English pronunciation practice fun, social, and authentic. Research into language acquisition suggests that exposure to natural, fast-spoken English in context is valuable for developing listening skills and pronunciation awareness. By watching and analysing clips from a popular TV series, learners engage with real spoken language in a low-stress, enjoyable environment.

Event Format

Tbl. 09 — Event Format09 / Extracurricular Event
Duration90 minutes
Target participantsB1–B2 level students (mixed)
Materials neededLaptop, projector, printed transcripts, whiteboard
FormatWatch clip → highlight connected speech features on whiteboard → choral and individual drill → role-play

Procedure

  1. Warm-up (10 min): Participants watch a 2-minute clip without subtitles and note what they understood.
  2. Analysis (20 min): Using printed transcripts, the facilitator highlights features of connected speech (linking, weak forms, contractions, assimilation). These are written on the board.
  3. Drilling (20 min): Participants practise selected lines chorally and individually using backchaining and repetition drills.
  4. Role-Play (30 min): In pairs, participants choose a short scene, practise it, and perform it for the group.
  5. Reflection (10 min): Brief group discussion — what surprised you? What will you listen for next time?

Evaluation

Participants complete a short feedback form rating enjoyment, perceived usefulness, and preferred elements. The facilitator uses this to plan future events.

StageProcedure / ActivityTimingInteraction
Lead-in T shows pictures of interesting life experiences (e.g. skydiving, eating unusual food). Ss discuss in pairs: Have you done any of these things? 5 min S-S, T-S
